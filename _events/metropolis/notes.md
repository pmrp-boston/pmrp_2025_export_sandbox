I am so excited to have had the opportunity to direct this show\! My
main reason for wanting to direct <cite>Metropolis</cite> was to
celebrate this spectacularly important piece of film, literary, and
cultural history coming into the public domain in the United States,
after [so much longer than was expected when it was
written](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_copyright_law_of_the_United_States).

<cite>Metropolis</cite> — both the book and especially the film — was so
hugely influential that it’s one of the foundational blocks upon which
modern film (and television), modern science fiction, and modern
cultural imagery around exploitation and oppression stand. Because of
this, I felt it was important to *translate* the 1927 film and novel
into a different medium, rather than telling a modern story, with modern
values, inspired by the original.

(In this translation, for instance, the film’s visual effects and sets
become sounds and soundscapes.)

One of the problems of copyright lasting almost a century is that when a
work comes into the public domain, its attitudes and values are almost a
hundred years old. Many of the cultural tropes reinforced, popularized,
or created by <cite>Metropolis</cite> are really bad, and I am very glad
that a lot of them have a weaker hold on our culture now than they did
in 1927. But they're also a part of <cite>Metropolis</cite>’ influence
on the culture and media that followed it. (And some of them, like the
Madonna–whore dichotomy
([tvtropes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/MadonnaWhoreComplex),
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna%E2%80%93whore_complex)),
and the shallow and cartoonish understanding of resistance to and
redress of oppression, are central to the story.) Taking the misogyny
and gender stereotyping, the notion that class distinctions are natural
and just need to be managed properly, and the sex-negativity completely
out of the story would result in a more truthful story, but it would be
a story that doesn’t explain <cite>Metropolis</cite>’ place in cultural
history. (Our version has a lot less of the fetishization and othering
of non-Western cultures, but it has *some* of that; for instance, a lot
of modern English-language references to Yoshiwara are to
<cite>Metropolis</cite>’s funhouse-mirror version, rather than to the
historical Yoshiwara district, so I wanted to at least leave a mention
of the name in.)

Despite all these problems (and
[Lang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Lang) and [von
Harbou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thea_von_Harbou)’s own deep
failings), the film version of <cite>Metropolis</cite> is a spectacular
and influential piece of art.

Among the cultural staples <cite>Metropolis</cite> had a huge role in
popularizing are:

  - Mad Scientists (along with Mary Shelley’s <cite>Frankenstein</cite>)
  - Robots taking human jobs
  - Robots appearing human and pretending to be human (à la
    <cite>Terminator</cite>, <cite>Westworld</cite>, or <cite>The
    Stepford Wives</cite>)
  - Evil doubles of good characters (which we can see in <cite>Invasion
    of the Body Snatchers</cite> or the <cite>Star Trek</cite> episode
    “Mirror, Mirror”)
  - Elite exploiters living aboveground, and brutally exploited workers
    living belowground (although [H. G. Wells’ Eloi and
    Morlocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Time_Machine) predate
    <cite>Metropolis</cite>)

And the iconic appearance of [the robot, or *Maschinenmensch*,
Futura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maschinenmensch) in the film
inspired the design for C3PO in <cite>Star Wars</cite>.

I hope you enjoy our production of <cite>Metropolis</cite>, and I hope
it helps tell the story of how film and science fiction, and the
cultures they are a part of, got where they are today.
